# iPhone Filmmaking in 30 Days

A beginner-to-working-level course for shooting handheld on an iPhone — no gimbal, no tripod, no mic, no lights. One lesson per day, ~30 minutes each: about 10 minutes of concept, about 20 minutes of actually filming. By Day 30 you will shoot and cut a proper scene with real coverage.

**Ground rules for the whole month:**

- Shoot in **4K at 24 fps** (Settings → Camera → Record Video). 24 fps is the "film look" frame rate; commit to it now so all your footage matches.
- Turn on the **Grid** (Settings → Camera → Grid). You'll use it every day.
- Keep every exercise clip. Make an album called "30 Days" — you'll edit from it in Weeks 3 and 4, and comparing Day 2 footage to Day 25 footage is the best motivation there is.
- Watch your footage back **the same day**, full screen, with the self-check questions. Shooting without reviewing is how people stay beginners.

## Week 1 — Seeing Like a Camera

### Day 1 — Take control of the camera
**The idea:** The iPhone constantly re-adjusts exposure and focus automatically, which looks amateur — brightness pumps mid-shot, focus hunts. Filmmakers lock these. In the Camera app, tap your subject to set focus/exposure, then **press and hold until "AE/AF LOCK" appears**. Slide your finger up/down next to the sun icon to brighten or darken. Rule of thumb: it's better to be slightly dark than to blow out highlights — you can lift shadows in an edit, but a white blown-out sky is gone forever.

**Exercise (20 min):** Film the same subject (a person or object near a window) three times, 15 seconds each: (1) full auto, moving the phone around; (2) AE/AF locked; (3) locked and deliberately exposed a touch darker to protect the window highlights. Move the phone during each take so you can see auto vs. locked behavior.

**Self-check:** In clip 1, can you see the brightness "breathing"? In clips 2–3, does it stay steady? Which one looks most like a movie?

### Day 2 — Handheld stability
**The idea:** You have no tripod, so your body is the tripod. The technique: elbows tucked hard against your ribs, phone gripped with both hands, strap of tension across your shoulders. Exhale slowly during the shot. For extra stability, lean against a wall or doorframe, or brace your elbows on a table, railing, or your knees while crouched. Keep clips short — nobody can hold steady for a minute, but anyone can for 10 seconds. Also turn on **Enhanced Stabilization** or use **Action Mode** for moving shots (know that Action Mode crops in and needs light).

**Exercise (20 min):** Film five 10-second static shots of different subjects around your home: (1) standing free, (2) leaning on a wall, (3) elbows braced on a surface, (4) crouched with elbows on knees, (5) phone pressed against a doorframe. Same subject distance each time.

**Self-check:** Rank the five for steadiness. You should be able to see the bracing hierarchy clearly. From now on, always find a brace.

### Day 3 — Shot sizes
**The idea:** The basic vocabulary of film. **Wide shot (WS):** the whole person/place — establishes where we are. **Medium shot (MS):** waist up — how people talk to each other. **Close-up (CU):** the face — emotion. **Extreme close-up (ECU):** eyes, hands, a detail — intensity or information. Crucially: change shot size by **moving your feet**, not by pinch-zooming. Digital zoom destroys quality; the 0.5x/1x/2x (or 3x/5x) lens buttons are fine because they're real lenses.

**Exercise (20 min):** Pick one subject (a person if possible, otherwise something like a kettle on the stove). Film 10 seconds each: WS, MS, CU, ECU — walking closer each time, using AE/AF lock and your Day 2 bracing. Then repeat the CU using the 2x/3x lens from further back and compare the look.

**Self-check:** Does each size feel like it "says" something different? Notice how the telephoto close-up compresses and flatters compared to shoving the wide lens in someone's face.

### Day 4 — Composition I: thirds, headroom, lead room
**The idea:** Put important things on the grid lines, not dead center. Eyes go on the **top third line**. **Headroom:** small gap above the head — too much makes people look like they're sinking. **Lead room:** when someone looks or moves sideways, leave space in front of their face/direction of travel, not behind their head. Center framing isn't forbidden — it reads as formal, confrontational, or symmetrical — but it should be a choice, not a default.

**Exercise (20 min):** Film a person (or a lamp with a distinct "face" direction) in a medium shot four ways, 10 seconds each: (1) dead center with lots of headroom (the classic beginner frame), (2) eyes on the top-third line, correct headroom, (3) subject on a side third looking *into* the empty space, (4) subject on a side third looking *out* of frame with the space behind them. 

**Self-check:** Frame 2 vs. 1: instantly more professional? Frame 4 should feel wrong/tense — remember that feeling, it's a tool you can use deliberately later.

### Day 5 — Composition II: depth and layers
**The idea:** A screen is flat; your job is to fake depth. Three tools: (1) **Foreground/midground/background** — shoot *through* things (a doorway, plants, a shoulder) so there's something soft near the lens; (2) **Leading lines** — roads, railings, table edges that point at your subject; (3) **Frame within a frame** — doorways, windows, mirrors, arches around your subject. Flat shot = subject against a bare wall. Deep shot = layers.

**Exercise (20 min):** Same subject, four 10-second shots: (1) flat against a wall (the control), (2) shot through a foreground object partially in frame, (3) positioned at the end of a leading line, (4) framed inside a doorway or window. Move around your home to find these — hunting for the frame IS the exercise.

**Self-check:** Compare 1 to the others. Depth should make the image feel bigger and more cinematic even though it's the same subject, same phone.

### Day 6 — Camera height and angle
**The idea:** Where you put the camera vertically is a statement. **Eye level:** neutral, honest. **Low angle** (camera below, looking up): subject looks powerful, imposing. **High angle** (looking down): subject looks small, vulnerable, or it becomes an overhead "god view" for showing layouts (food, desks, maps). **Dutch tilt** (horizon deliberately slanted): unease — use rarely. Beginners shoot everything from standing eye height; simply crouching is the fastest way to make footage more interesting.

**Exercise (20 min):** Film someone doing a simple action (pouring a drink, tying shoes) from: eye level, low angle (phone near the floor looking up), high angle, and directly overhead of just the hands/object. 10 seconds each. Phones are great at this — you can put the lens places a big camera can't go.

**Self-check:** Does the low angle genuinely change how the person "feels"? Which angle told the story of the action best?

### Day 7 — Build your first sequence
**The idea:** Consolidation day. A sequence is multiple shots of one event that cut together to compress time and direct attention. Today you combine everything: locked exposure, bracing, varied shot sizes, composition, varied angles — into one mini-film of a mundane task. The discipline: **every shot a different size or angle**. Two consecutive shots that are similar sizes from similar angles will "jump" when cut together.

**Exercise (25 min):** Film "making a hot drink" (or any 2-minute routine) in exactly six static shots, ~10 seconds each: e.g. (1) WS entering kitchen, (2) overhead ECU of kettle switch, (3) MS at counter, (4) low-angle CU of steam, (5) ECU of pouring, (6) WS or CU of first sip. Plan the six on paper first (two minutes), then shoot.

**Self-check:** Play the clips back-to-back in order in your camera roll. Even uncut, does it read as a little story? Could a stranger follow what happened?

## Week 2 — Movement and Light

### Day 8 — The pan and the tilt
**The idea:** A pan (rotating horizontally) and tilt (vertically) are the only moves that don't require walking. Handheld rules: (1) **rotate from your hips/torso**, not your wrists — pre-twist your body toward the END position and unwind into the shot, so you finish relaxed and steady; (2) know your **start frame and end frame** before rolling — a pan is a sentence with a beginning and end, not a security-camera sweep; (3) go slower than feels natural, then slower again; (4) hold the start still for 2 seconds, move, hold the end still for 2 seconds — those holds make it usable in an edit.

**Exercise (20 min):** (1) Pan from an empty doorway to a person across the room — 3 takes, each slower. (2) Tilt from someone's shoes up to their face. (3) A "reveal" pan: start on a detail (a photo, a cup), pan to reveal the wider scene it belongs to.

**Self-check:** Are your start/end frames each well-composed shots in their own right? Is the motion smooth or wobbly-wristed? The reveal pan should feel like it delivers information.

### Day 9 — Push in, pull out
**The idea:** Walking toward or away from your subject while filming. A slow **push-in** builds intensity or focus (think: realization on a face). A **pull-out** ends things, reveals context, or leaves someone alone in a space. Technique — the "ninja walk": knees bent, feet rolling heel-to-toe, phone held slightly away from your body so your arms absorb bounce like a suspension system, small steps. Enable Action Mode for these if there's enough light; compare with it off.

**Exercise (20 min):** (1) Push-in from a wide room shot to a close-up of an object on a table — 3 takes: normal walk (feel the bounce), ninja walk, ninja walk with Action Mode. (2) Pull-out from a person's face to a wide shot of them alone in the room. Keep pushes/pulls to 5–8 seconds.

**Self-check:** How much did the ninja walk kill the vertical bounce? Does the push-in create a feeling of "pay attention to THIS"?

### Day 10 — Tracking a moving subject
**The idea:** Following someone as they move. Three classic handheld tracks: **follow** (behind them, seeing what they see), **lead** (walking backward in front of them — have a spotter or clear path!), and **side track** (walking parallel). Keep the subject's position consistent in frame — pick where they sit on the grid and hold them there; your legs do the stabilizing, your arms do the fine correction. Let the subject, not the camera, be the thing that moves through the world.

**Exercise (20 min):** Recruit anyone (or film a pet, or set your phone following your own feet). Get: (1) 10-second follow shot down a hallway or sidewalk, (2) 10-second side track, (3) lead shot if safe. Two takes each, second take slower and more deliberate.

**Self-check:** Does the subject stay glued to the same spot in frame, or drift around? Drift = your walking rhythm leaking into your arms.

### Day 11 — The orbit and the reveal
**The idea:** Two moves that add production value cheaply. **Orbit/arc:** walk a half-circle around a stationary subject, keeping them centered and the same size in frame — the background sweeps behind them, which looks expensive. **Movement reveal:** start with the lens blocked or aimed at a foreground object, then move so the real subject is revealed (slide out from behind a wall, rise from behind a counter, drift past a doorframe). Reveals are how you turn a boring "here's a room" shot into a moment.

**Exercise (20 min):** (1) Orbit a seated person or an object on a table: half-circle, 10 seconds, 3 takes — the challenge is keeping distance constant (imagine a rope tying you to the subject). (2) Two different reveals: emerge from behind a doorframe to reveal the room; start on a close object and slide sideways to reveal a person behind it.

**Self-check:** In the orbit, does the subject stay the same size, or do you drift in/out? Do the reveals create a small "oh!" beat?

### Day 12 — Light I: direction and quality
**The idea:** You own no lights, so your skill is *positioning people relative to existing light*. Two properties matter. **Direction:** front light (flat, boring), **side light** (shape, drama — the go-to), back light (silhouette or halo). **Quality:** hard light (direct sun/bare bulb — sharp shadows) vs. **soft light** (window with no direct sun, cloudy day — flattering, gentle shadows). The biggest free upgrade in filmmaking: put your subject next to a big window, turned at 45° to it.

**Exercise (20 min):** Put a person (or your own face, front camera is fine) near a window and film 15 seconds each: (1) facing the window (front light), (2) window at 90° to their side, (3) window behind them, exposure locked on their face, (4) window behind them, exposure locked on the window (silhouette). Note whether your window light today is hard or soft.

**Self-check:** Side light should look the most dimensional — see how one side of the face falls off into shadow? The silhouette and the halo are both usable *choices*.

### Day 13 — Light II: golden hour and outdoors
**The idea:** The hour after sunrise and before sunset is the cheapest cinematography money can't buy: low, warm, soft-ish directional light. Midday sun is the enemy — top-down hard light puts raccoon shadows in eye sockets. Midday survival tactics: put subjects in **open shade** (shadow of a building, under a tree) facing out toward the light, or use backlight and expose for the face. Overcast days are a giant free softbox — flat but flattering.

**Exercise (20 min — time it to golden hour if you possibly can):** Outdoors, film a person or interesting object: (1) side-lit by the low sun, (2) backlit with the sun creating a rim/halo, exposed for the subject, (3) in open shade facing the open sky. If you can only shoot midday, do the open-shade versions and one direct-sun shot to see exactly why it's ugly.

**Self-check:** Backlit golden hour should look like a commercial. Compare skin/texture in open shade vs. direct sun.

### Day 14 — Light III: low light and night
**The idea:** Phones struggle in the dark: noise, mush, and slow shutter smear. The fixes are about working *with* the phone: (1) find the light that exists — streetlamps, shop windows, screens, stove hood — and put your subject IN it, don't film them beside it; (2) expose slightly under rather than letting the phone brighten everything into noise; (3) brace harder than usual (Day 2 skills) because stabilization struggles at night; (4) practical lights *in frame* (lamps, signs, fairy lights, a phone screen lighting a face) look great and hide the noise in atmosphere.

**Exercise (20 min, after dark):** (1) Film a person lit only by a lamp, positioned so the lamp is IN the shot. (2) Film a face lit by a phone/laptop screen. (3) Go to a window/streetlight and film someone stepping from darkness into the pool of light. Lock exposure on the lit side of the face each time.

**Self-check:** Which shots hide the noise best? You should find that "small pools of light in darkness" beats "everything dimly visible" every time.

## Week 3 — Film Grammar: Shooting to Edit

### Day 15 — Coverage and the five-shot method
**The idea:** From here on, you stop shooting *shots* and start shooting *scenes*. "Coverage" means getting enough different shots of an event to edit it. The classic five-shot method for any action: (1) **CU of the hands** doing the thing, (2) **CU of the face**, (3) **wide shot** showing person + place, (4) **over-the-shoulder** showing the action from behind the person, (5) **one creative shot** — low angle, orbit, through a doorway, your choice. Five shots of anything cuts into a watchable sequence.

**Exercise (25 min):** Pick an action with texture — chopping vegetables, sketching, repotting a plant, cleaning a bike. Shoot the five shots, ~15 seconds each, having your subject repeat the action for each setup (or restart it yourself). Different size/angle every shot, per Day 7.

**Self-check:** Could you cut these five into a 30-second piece where the viewer understands who, where, what, and how it feels? That question is now your standard for every scene.

### Day 16 — Continuity and cutting on action
**The idea:** To cut invisibly between two shots, cut **during a movement** — a door opening, a cup being lifted, someone sitting down. The eye follows the motion across the cut and doesn't notice the edit. This requires shooting the **same action twice from two different setups**, with the action **overlapping** in both takes (film the whole action both times, not half and half). Also: keep continuity — same hand, same objects in the same places, roughly the same speed.

**Exercise (20 min):** Film someone (or your own hands) performing one complete action twice: e.g. picking up a mug and drinking. Take 1: medium shot, full action. Take 2: close-up, full action, matching hand and speed. Do this for two different actions. If you have an editor (see Day 21), try the cut tonight: cut from the MS to the CU mid-lift.

**Self-check:** Did the subject genuinely repeat the action the same way? Mismatched hands and teleporting mugs are the #1 continuity bust.

### Day 17 — The 180° rule and screen direction
**The idea:** Draw an imaginary line between two people talking (or along a subject's direction of travel). Keep your camera on **one side of that line** for the whole scene. Cross it, and person A suddenly looks left instead of right — the audience's mental map breaks and it feels like the characters swapped places. Same for movement: if someone exits frame right, they should enter the next shot from frame left, still traveling the same screen direction.

**Exercise (20 min):** Set up two chairs facing each other (use a person + a propped-up jacket if you're solo). (1) Film an over-the-shoulder of each "person" from the SAME side of the line — notice A looks right, B looks left: they connect. (2) Now deliberately break it: shoot B from the wrong side — both now look the same direction. (3) Film someone walking through a door left-to-right, then film them continuing in the next room left-to-right, then a wrong version right-to-left.

**Self-check:** Watch the correct pair, then the broken pair. You should physically feel the broken one being wrong. That instinct is the lesson.

### Day 18 — Shot / reverse shot: covering a conversation
**The idea:** The bread and butter of narrative filmmaking. Standard conversation coverage: (1) a **master** — a wide two-shot of both people, entire conversation; (2) **over-the-shoulder (OTS) on A** — foreground shoulder of B framing A's face; (3) **OTS on B** from the same side of the line (Day 17!); optionally (4) **clean singles** (close-ups with no shoulder). Match the two OTS shots: same size, same eye height, mirrored composition. Run the *whole* conversation in every setup so you can cut anywhere.

**Exercise (25 min):** Get anyone to have a 30-second scripted-ish exchange with you or a friend ("What do you want for dinner?" works). Shoot: master two-shot, OTS on each person, one clean CU. If you're solo, stage it with a stand-in object and shoot the coverage anyway, playing both parts — the framing skills transfer completely.

**Self-check:** Are the two OTS frames mirror images in size and eye position? Do the eyelines cross (A looks frame-right, B looks frame-left)?

### Day 19 — Sound
**The idea:** Audiences forgive ugly video but not bad audio. With no mic, your rules: (1) **distance is everything** — the phone's mics need to be within ~1 m of a speaking person, so frame accordingly (this is why close coverage matters) or shoot wide for picture and grab a separate close audio take with the Voice Memos app to sync later; (2) kill background noise at the source — fridge, fan, AC, music off; (3) never cover the mics with your grip; (4) record 30 seconds of **room tone** (silence of the space) at every location — editors use it to patch gaps; (5) wind outdoors is fatal — block it with your body or shoot elsewhere.

**Exercise (20 min):** Record someone (or yourself) speaking a fixed sentence: (1) from 3 m, (2) from 1 m, (3) from 1 m with the fridge/fan you never noticed turned OFF. Then record 30 s of room tone. Outdoors if possible: one clip into the wind, one with your body blocking it. Listen on headphones.

**Self-check:** The 3 m vs. 1 m difference should shock you. Can you hear how much machine hum you'd stopped noticing?

### Day 20 — B-roll and inserts
**The idea:** B-roll is every shot that isn't your main subject: the hands, the environment, the details, the reactions. It has two jobs: **covering cuts** (when you shorten an interview or scene, the cutaway hides the jump) and **carrying meaning** (a shot of an untouched coffee going cold says "she waited a long time" without dialogue). Shoot b-roll like a hunter: details that *tell*, not random pretty stuff. Rule of thumb on any shoot: for every minute of main footage, grab five detail shots.

**Exercise (20 min):** Choose a person or a place with a story (a busy kitchen, your desk mid-project, a shopkeeper). Shoot 10 b-roll shots, 8–10 seconds each, that together describe it WITHOUT ever showing a face saying anything: hands, textures, wide establishers, details, one shot implying time passing.

**Self-check:** Show the 10 clips to someone and ask what story they add up to. If they get it, your b-roll speaks.

### Day 21 — Edit day I: assemble a sequence
**The idea:** Editing teaches shooting better than anything — you instantly discover which shots you *wished* you'd gotten. Install **CapCut or iMovie** (both free) on the phone. Today's craft: (1) cut the dead air off the head and tail of every clip; (2) cut on action (Day 16); (3) vary shot sizes across cuts (Day 7); (4) shorter than feels right — 3–5 seconds a shot is plenty; (5) no transitions — hard cuts only; dissolves are a crutch.

**Exercise (25 min):** Edit your Day 15 five-shot coverage (or Day 7 sequence) into a 30–45 second piece. Then — the important part — write down the ONE shot you wish you had, go film it now, and drop it into the cut.

**Self-check:** Does anything feel like it "jumps"? (Similar sizes cut together — Day 7.) Did the missing-shot exercise sting? That pain is what "shooting for the edit" means; from now on you'll feel the edit while shooting.

## Week 4 — Real Situations and the Final Scene

### Day 22 — Frame rates: slow motion and timelapse
**The idea:** Deviating from 24 fps is a storytelling choice about time. **Slo-mo** (shoot 120/240 fps): reveals detail and emotion in motion — splashes, hair, fabric, a smile forming. It needs **lots of light** (high frame rate = fast shutter = dark, noisy footage indoors) and works best on things that actually move. **Timelapse** (the Camera app mode): compresses minutes into seconds — clouds, crowds, shadows crawling, a task completed. For timelapse, handheld is out — *brace the phone against something rigid* (propped on a ledge, leaned against books).

**Exercise (20 min):** (1) Shoot three slo-mo clips outdoors or by a bright window: water pouring, someone jumping or turning, fabric/hair in wind. (2) Set the phone against a solid object and timelapse 5–10 minutes of something changing (sky, street, you tidying a room — walk in and out of frame).

**Self-check:** Compare a bright slo-mo clip to one shot in a dim room — see the noise/flicker? File away: slo-mo is a daylight tool.

### Day 23 — Cinematic Mode and rack focus
**The idea:** Cinematic Mode fakes the shallow depth of field of big cameras and — more importantly — lets you **rack focus**: shift focus from one subject to another mid-shot, which is a director's tool for steering attention ("she's talking… but look at his reaction"). Tap subjects while rolling to pull focus; you can even re-do focus choices after the fact in the Photos edit screen. Its limits: struggles with fast motion, complex edges (hair, glasses), and low light — treat it as a special tool, not the default.

**Exercise (20 min):** In Cinematic Mode: (1) frame a person in the foreground and an object/person in the background, and rack focus between them twice mid-shot; (2) shoot a slow push-in on a face; (3) shoot the same setup in normal video and compare honestly. Then open a clip in Photos → Edit and change a focus decision in post.

**Self-check:** Where does the fake blur break (look at hair edges, cup rims)? When did it genuinely elevate the shot? Now you know when to reach for it.

### Day 24 — The interview / talking head
**The idea:** The most-shot setup on earth, combining half the course: sit your subject at 45° to a window (Day 12), frame a medium close-up with eyes on the top third (Day 4), have them look at YOU — position your face right beside the lens — not into the lens (unless you want to-camera address), phone within 1 m for sound (Day 19), background with depth, not a bare wall (Day 5). Ask questions that can't be answered yes/no, and have them fold the question into the answer ("What did you eat?" → "This morning I ate…").

**Exercise (25 min):** Interview anyone for 2 minutes about something they love. Set up properly: window light, thirds, depth behind them, close enough for audio, room tone after. Then grab 5 b-roll shots of the thing they talked about (Day 20).

**Self-check:** Eyes bright and lit? Eyeline close to the lens but not in it? Audio clean on headphones? Could you cut the b-roll over their voice?

### Day 25 — Action and movement
**The idea:** Fast subjects (kids, sports, pets, traffic) break polite technique, so the approach changes: (1) **let the subject cross the frame** — a static braced camera with a cyclist ripping through is more dynamic than a flailing pan; (2) when you must move, use Action Mode and commit to big smooth moves, not corrections; (3) get **more angles than feels necessary** — action edits are fast, 1–2 seconds a shot; (4) whip-pan with the motion as a transition; (5) low angles make anything faster and more heroic; (6) shoot some of it in slo-mo (Day 22) for the highlight moments.

**Exercise (25 min):** Film 60 seconds of any fast action — someone running/cycling past, a dog fetching, kids playing, even traffic: (1) static wide letting subject cross frame, (2) low-angle static, (3) tracking shot alongside, (4) whip-pan following them past you, (5) one slo-mo pass. Multiple passes of the same action = free coverage.

**Self-check:** Compare the static "subject crosses frame" shot to your tracking attempt — often the static one wins. Do your five angles cut into an energetic mini-sequence?

### Day 26 — Run and gun: difficult real-world conditions
**The idea:** Weddings, streets, events, travel — no second takes. The discipline: (1) **arrive at the shot before the moment** — anticipate where action will happen and be framed up waiting; (2) shoot the Day 15 five-shot pattern on autopilot for every situation; (3) hold every shot 10 full seconds even when nervous — count silently; (4) in mixed/ugly light, expose for faces and let the rest go; (5) in crowds, get high (arms up, steps) for overview and low/close for intimacy — the mid-height mush in between is where boring footage lives; (6) grab room tone and ambient sound between moments.

**Exercise (25 min):** Go somewhere alive — market, park, café, busy street. In 25 minutes, come back with a five-shot story of the place plus five b-roll details, all usable: steady, composed, well-exposed. No do-overs allowed: treat every take as the only take.

**Self-check:** How many of your ten shots are genuinely usable? (Honest count.) Where did you get caught moving when the moment happened?

### Day 27 — Blocking and staging
**The idea:** So far the camera moved and subjects mostly didn't. Directors think in **blocking**: choreographing where subjects move *within* a frame. Tools: (1) **depth blocking** — subject walks from background to foreground (or away), changing their own shot size with zero camera movement; (2) crossing frame to motivate a pan; (3) two people rebalancing a frame as one sits/stands/turns away; (4) motivated camera — the camera only moves when a subject's movement "pulls" it. One well-blocked shot can replace three cuts — this is how you'll design your final scene.

**Exercise (25 min):** With a person (or walking through frame yourself with the phone braced on a shelf): (1) subject starts deep in the room, walks up to a close-up, delivers a line — camera never moves; (2) subject crosses the room, your pan follows, they settle into a new composed frame; (3) two "characters" (person + stand-in): one turns away to a window mid-conversation — recompose the frame as they move.

**Self-check:** Shot 1 should feel like a "real movie shot" — that's blocking doing the work of cutting. Did your pan in shot 2 land on a composed end frame (Day 8)?

### Day 28 — Write the scene, build the shot list
**The idea:** Pre-production. Pick a simple two-person scene, 30–60 seconds, one location, with a tiny arc: a want, a bit of friction, a resolution. (Steal these if stuck: someone asks to borrow money; a birthday present goes wrong; two people both grab the last slice; someone confesses they broke a thing.) Write half a page of dialogue. Then build a **shot list**: master two-shot, OTS both ways, clean CU on each, one insert (an object that matters), one blocking or camera move that earns its place. Sketch each frame as stick figures — that's a storyboard. Decide your light source and time of day (Days 12–13).

**Exercise (25 min):** Write the scene (10 min). Build the shot list and stick-figure storyboard (10 min). Then scout: stand in the actual location, look through the phone at each planned frame, and film a 10-second lighting/framing test of the two key setups (5 min).

**Self-check:** Does every shot on the list have a job? Can the whole list be shot from one side of the 180° line (Day 17)? Is your location quiet (Day 19)?

### Day 29 — Shoot the scene
**The idea:** Production day. The professional order: block it (walk it through with your actors), rehearse once, then shoot **setup by setup** — all takes of the master first, then move to each OTS, then CUs, then the insert — running the *full scene every take* so you can cut anywhere. Two takes minimum per setup. Direct with playback: watch a take with your actors, adjust, go again. Watch continuity between takes (Day 16), keep the eyeline sides consistent (Days 17–18), stay within 1 m for close audio coverage (Day 19), and record room tone before wrapping.

**Exercise (30 min):** Shoot your Day 28 scene, complete coverage per your shot list. If actors fall through, shoot it anyway playing both parts, or make it a phone-call scene with one visible actor — the coverage discipline is the point.

**Self-check (on set):** After each setup ask: exposure locked? framing on thirds? eyeline correct side? audio close enough? Got two clean takes? Only then move on.

### Day 30 — Cut the scene, judge the month
**The idea:** Post-production and graduation. Cut the scene in CapCut/iMovie: lay the master down as a spine, then replace sections with OTS/CU coverage, cutting on action, saving the tightest close-up for the scene's key beat, dropping the insert where it lands hardest. Aim for 45–75 seconds. Then the graduation exercise: watch your Day 2 clips, then this scene, back to back.

**Exercise (30 min):** Edit the scene (20 min). Then the working-level self-assessment (10 min) — score yourself honestly 1–5 on each: steady braced shots · locked exposure · shot-size variety · composition with depth · using found light · clean audio · correct eyelines/180° · coverage that cuts. Anything under 3 tells you exactly which day to repeat.

**Where to go from here:** Repeat Days 15–30 monthly with new scenes — coverage is a muscle. Your first gear purchases, in order of impact: a $20 clamp tripod, a $30 lavalier mic, and only much later a gimbal — you now have handheld skills most gimbal owners never build. And keep the habit that made this month work: **never shoot without watching it back the same day.**

## Quick-reference card

- **Settings:** 4K/24 fps, grid on, AE/AF lock on every shot, expose for highlights/faces.
- **Stability:** elbows in, brace on something, 10-second shots, exhale.
- **Every scene:** five-shot method — hands CU, face CU, wide, OTS, creative.
- **Composition:** eyes on top third, lead room, shoot through foreground for depth.
- **Light:** subject at 45° to a window; outdoors use golden hour or open shade; at night, pools of light with the source in frame.
- **Sound:** within 1 m, kill the hum, block the wind, record room tone.
- **Editing grammar:** one side of the 180° line, cut on action, vary sizes, hard cuts only.
